Australia's Bushfires: From Control to Catastrophe

A data-driven exploration of bushfire activity across Australia (2016-2021), highlighting the shift from planned burns to widespread unplanned infernos that ultimately culminated in the Black Summer of 2019-2020.

Overview

Controlled burning is one of Australia's most important land management tools. It reduces fuel loads, protects habitats, and prevents larger fires. However, over the years 2016-2021, the balance between planned and unplanned fires changed dramatically. This story visualises that shift across multiple dimensions: spatial, temporal, and intensity, with an increased focus on the build-up to the Black Summer period of September 2019 to January 2020, using accurate data reports and figures.

1) Where Unplanned Fires Burned the Most

Spatial distribution of burns across Australia.

As can be ascertained from the visualization, states like Queensland and Northern Territory have also been prone to hosting unplanned bushfires, at a number much more than other Australian states. As expected, around the period 2019-20, both Queensland and Northern Territory experienced an increase in unplanned bushfires, along with New South Wales and Victoria reaching numbers they had never reached before.

Regions of Australia with arid interiors have always burned more in the summertime, yet, the recordings during and leading up to the Black Summer revealed how rising temperatures and dry fuel loads brought bushfire risk closer to the populated communities of Australia.

2) Planned vs Unplanned Fires: Changing Balance

Comparison of bushfire kind across different years.

Australia's strategy once revolved around planned bushfires - delibrate, low-intensity fires to manage fuel loads and prevent catastrophic burnings. As conditions worsened however, unplanned bushfires began to dominate. Observing from 2018 to 2019, we see a change in the number of unplanned bushfires / total bushfires from 48% to 54%. This then increases to 58% in 2020, clearly visualizing the increase.

This shift in the share of unplanned bushfires in total bushfires signals how climate change managed to outpace traditional control methods, decreasing the effective of prevention when it was most needed.

3) State-by-State Trends in Unplanned and Planned Fires

Line chart showchasing bushfire trends for each Australian state

In the Northern states, unplanned bushfire numbers have occupied most of Australia's unplanned bushfires. As the trend can be observed from the beginning of 2019, both Northern states recorded an increase in unplanned bushfires and therefore, a decreased in planned ones. A surprise was the rapid increase of unplanned bushfires in the South-East of Australia, notably New South Wales and Victoria, peaking in the window of late 2019 and early 2020.

After the summer period in early 2020, the Australian government's response to the Black Summer resulted in a decrease in unplanned bushfires across all states with an increase in planned ones, further limiting the possibility of enduring another Black Summer.

The synchrony across multiple states signified how multiple factors (heat, drought, wind) all came into play in a way that overwhelmed Australia's fire management resources and bushfire preparations. Since then, Australia hasn't faced such a catastrophic event, mostly owing to their increased importance on bushfire preparations.

4) The Black Summer on the Ground

Fire Hotspot Map populated with accurate data from VIIRS

The chart accurately represents the number of occurences of bushfires captured by the VIIRS satellite as well as the reliability of the occurrence. Each point is spread over a 20 km radius which encapsulates all occurrences. The data is modelled from Sep 2019 - Jan 2020.

In the months of September, October and November, the majority of hotspots are observed in the Northern states, with activity increasing in the South-East as the months progress. It's most notable in December that bushfires progress to an unprecedent rate, especially in the South-East states, a densely populated region that hasn't experienced bushfires of this magnitude before. The data from January 2020 supports our conclusion that the Australian government, aided by changing natural parameters, was able to put a stop to the catastrophic burnings and subdue the Black Summer.

5) Rising Fire Intensity: High Confidence Detections

This steamgraph compares the number of detections classified as High to detections classified as Nominal/Other.

The stream chart supports the findings and conclusions of the previous visualizations by modelling the increase in firebush detections from September 2019, with the peak being reached in November 2019. From there, detections decreased, mirroring the rise and fall of the Black Summer.

The chart clearly displays the catastrophe that ocurred over the 5 months between September 2019 and January 2020. Scattered events turned into a nation-wide emergency, with the country facing unprecendented odds. These visualizations serve to enforce the effects of the Black Summer and in general, catastrophic bushfires.

Conclusion

Between 2016 to 2021, Australia's approach to bushfire management received its greatest test. Planned burns either remained steady or decreased, while unplanned fires surged, culminating in the Black Summer. These visualizations not only showcase when and where fires burned, but how the balance between prevention and catastrophe shifted, leaving an indellible mark on the Australian landscape.
